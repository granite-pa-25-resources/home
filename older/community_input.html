Community Subcommittee

The district created a Community Subcommittee with over 40 members including:

  • 2 parent SCC representatives per school (20 parents total)
  • 1 teacher representative per school (10 teachers)
  • City council representatives from Millcreek and Holladay

Evidence of Community Impact on Decisions

ALC Consolidation Reversals (September-October 2025)

The PAC's initial recommendation (presented September 2) proposed consolidating all east-side Advanced Learner Centers at Woodstock Elementary. After intense community feedback:

  • September 19: District completely reversed - Morningside ALC would remain separate from Woodstock but relocate to a new site
  • October 21: District further clarified - Morningside ALC will remain at Morningside Elementary (no relocation)

What This Shows: Community input absolutely can influence PAC recommendations. The two reversals demonstrate that the Board and PAC are responsive to parent concerns when feedback is strong and organized.

Parent-Led Survey Results (October 2025)

A grassroots parent-led survey collected responses from 84 Area 5 families (October 9-13, 2025). The survey reveals strong community preferences and concerns about the boundary study:

Source: Area 5 Parent Led Survey (Oct 9-13, 2025) - Grassroots community survey, 84 respondents. Note: This is an independent parent-led survey, not an official district document.

Survey Demographics

  • Total Respondents: 84 families
  • Current Elementary Students: 77% (65 families)
  • Geographic Distribution: Majority from 4500-3900 South corridor (57%)

Current School Attendance (73 responses)

  • Howard R. Driggs: 64.4% (47 families)
  • Morningside: 16.4% (12 families)
  • Eastwood: 4.1% (3 families)
  • Other schools: 15.1% (11 families)

First Choice School for 2026-2027 (80 responses)

Overwhelming Preference for Driggs:

  • Howard R. Driggs: 71.3% (57 families) - Highest preference by far
  • Morningside: 13.8% (11 families)
  • Eastwood: 3.8% (3 families)
  • Crestview: 2.5% (2 families)
  • Other options: 8.8% (7 families including private/charter)

Key Finding: 71% of respondents want Driggs as their first choice, far exceeding any other school. This suggests that boundary changes moving students away from Driggs will face significant resistance.

Second and Third Choice Preferences

  • Second Choice Leaders: Crestview (35%), Canyon Rim Academy (19%), Driggs (14%)
  • Third Choice Distribution: More diverse - Cottonwood (18%), Crestview (16%), Private School (12%), Driggs (12%)
  • Notable: 16% of third-choice responses included "homeschool," "leave district," or "none" - suggesting some families may exit public education if boundary changes don't align with preferences

Barriers to Boundary School Attendance (52 responses)

Parents who would not choose their assigned boundary school cited multiple concerns:

Top Concerns About Cottonwood Elementary:

  • Distance/Location: "Too far away" (multiple respondents)
  • School Quality: "Cottonwood is not a quality school"
  • Facility Concerns: "Old building," "struggling school"
  • Traffic Safety: "Pick-up and drop off... terrible and dangerous with it being right on a busy road"
  • Long-term Viability: "Instability of staying open long term"
  • Administration: "Concerning based on... previous leadership at Crestview"

Reasons for Staying at Current School:

  • Community/Friends: Mentioned by 15+ respondents
  • Class Size: Multiple mentions of concerns about larger classes
  • School Stability: "We don't want our kids having to leave a school they are comfortable with"
  • Program Continuity: DLI-French, ALC programs
  • Walkability: "Being able to walk/ride our bikes safely to school is a priority"

Parent Suggestions to the District (63 responses)

Most Common Recommendations:

  1. Keep Driggs Open: Mentioned in 20+ responses
    • "Keep Driggs! It is a great school!"
    • "Leave Driggs boundaries alone"
    • "Put the ALC at Cottonwood and keep Driggs boundary the same"
  2. Move Programs to Cottonwood: 15+ suggestions
    • "Move Morningside ALC and traditional to Cottonwood"
    • "Send the ALC kids to Cottonwood Elementary"
    • "Close Cottonwood or make it the stand-alone DLI school"
  3. Listen to Parents: 10+ responses
    • "Actually listen to what the parents are complaining/worried about"
    • "Listen to the parents! My biggest worry is that... the wrong changes will make the problem worse"
  4. Take More Time: Multiple requests
    • "Take another year to continue the study"
    • "This study feels very rushed and not backed by data"
    • "I think you all need to take another year and make a better decision"
  5. Keep Neighborhoods Together: Repeated theme
    • "Keep neighborhoods together"
    • "We don't want our neighborhood to be split between 2 schools"

Survey Analysis & Implications

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Disconnect:

The parent-led survey reveals a major mismatch between PAC recommendations and parent preferences:

  • 71% want Driggs as their first choice, yet PAC boundary changes would move some Driggs families to Cottonwood
  • Zero families selected Cottonwood as first, second, or third choice (excluding "other" write-ins)
  • Oakridge received zero first or second choice votes despite being recommended to stay open
  • Multiple families threatened to leave the district entirely if forced to attend Cottonwood

Risk: If boundary changes don't align with parent preferences, the district may see:

  • Increased open enrollment applications (families permitting to non-boundary schools)
  • Exodus to charter/private schools (multiple survey respondents mentioned this)
  • Continued enrollment decline at schools parents don't want (Cottonwood, Oakridge)
  • Administrative burden from managing high permit volumes

Survey Limitations:

  • Sample Size: 84 responses is small relative to ~4,000 Area 5 students
  • Self-Selection Bias: Survey likely over-represents engaged Driggs families (64% of current attendees)
  • Geographic Bias: 51% from 4500-3900 South between 2300-2700 East (Driggs area)
  • Underrepresentation: Very few responses from Cottonwood, Oakridge, Penn, or Oakwood families

Despite these limitations, the survey provides valuable qualitative data on parent concerns that official district surveys may not capture. The intensity of preference for Driggs and aversion to Cottonwood is notable.
