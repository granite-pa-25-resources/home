Granite School District Area 5 Study

Independent Analysis of the 2025 Boundary and Closure Study

Analysis Date: January 2025

🔔 IMPORTANT UPDATE (Sept 19, 2025):

The district REVERSED its ALC consolidation plan. Morningside ALC will relocate to a new location (TBD) but remain separate from Woodstock ALC.

Executive Summary

The Granite School District is conducting a Population Analysis Study for Area 5, which includes 10 elementary schools on the east side of the district. The study is driven by declining enrollment trends affecting the entire district over the past 15+ years.

District Enrollment Decline

2010: 68,614 students

2024: 55,546 students

Decline: -19.0%

2029 Projection: 49,296 students (-28% from 2010)

Area 5 Current Status (2024-25)

Total Students: ~3,980

Schools: 10 elementary schools

Average per School: 398 students

Target Average: 500-550 students

PAC Recommendations

Eastwood: Close & merge with Oakridge

Morningside: Convert to French DLI standalone (boundary closure)

Timeline: Final decision Dec 2025, implementation Fall 2026

Goal Alignment Analysis: Target Average vs PAC Recommendations

Critical Finding: PAC Recommendations Do NOT Meet Stated Goals

The district's stated goal is to achieve 500-550 students per school. However, the PAC recommendations fail to achieve this target and contain significant mathematical inconsistencies.

Current Math (2024-25 Actual Total Enrollment):

  • 10 schools, ~3,980 students = 398 avg/school (below target)

After PAC Recommendations (Projected Impact):

  • Eastwood closes: 267 students redistributed
  • Morningside boundary closes: ~309 in-boundary students redistributed (French DLI program remains with ~250-300 choice students)
  • Result: 8 boundary schools + 1 standalone DLI program
  • Available students for 8 boundary schools: 3,980 - 250 (Morningside DLI) = ~3,730
  • New average: 3,730 ÷ 8 = 466 students/school

GOAL NOT MET: 466 < 500-550 Target Range

Even after closing two schools and redistributing approximately 576 students, the resulting average of 466 students per school is still 34-84 students below the target range. The PAC recommendations fail to achieve the district's stated capacity optimization goal.

Why the Plan Falls Short:

Methodological Flaw #1

In-Boundary vs Total Enrollment Mismatch

The study uses in-boundary resident forecasts (showing decline) but schools actually operate on total enrollment (includes choice students). Many schools have 50-80% MORE students than their boundary populations.

Methodological Flaw #2

Insufficient Consolidation

To reach 500-550 avg with ~3,980 total students would require reducing to 7-8 schools. The PAC recommends only 2 closures (to 8 schools), which mathematically cannot achieve the target.

Methodological Flaw #3

Choice Program Paradox

Morningside's closure as a boundary school while retaining it as a DLI program creates administrative complexity without addressing capacity issues. The DLI program will still occupy the building with 250-300 students.

Alternative Scenario to Actually Meet Goals:

To reach 500 students/school average with 3,980 total students: 3,980 ÷ 500 = ~8 schools needed (matching PAC plan)

To reach 550 students/school average: 3,980 ÷ 550 = ~7 schools needed (would require 3 closures)

Conclusion on Goal Alignment

The PAC recommendations partially address capacity concerns but do NOT meet the district's stated target average of 500-550 students per school. The plan achieves only 466 students/school, falling short by 7-15%. To truly meet stated goals, the district would need to:

  • Close one additional school (3 total closures instead of 2), OR
  • Revise the target average to 450-500 students/school to match realistic outcomes, OR
  • Address the choice-based enrollment discrepancy by limiting out-of-boundary transfers

The current plan represents a compromise between community concerns and capacity optimization rather than a data-driven solution aligned with stated goals.

How the Study is Being Conducted

Study Process Overview

  1. December - February: Population Analysis Committee (PAC) reviews demographic data from multiple sources including MGT forecasts and historical enrollment
  2. February - June: PAC meets monthly; community input collected through councils and public forums
  3. June - July: PAC presents initial report to Board of Education with preliminary recommendations
  4. July - August: Board refinement based on input from Board of Education and community feedback
  5. September: PAC holds public open houses; community input collected
  6. October - December: Board guidance and community feedback shape final recommendations presented for approval in public hearings (Nov 18, Dec 2, 2025)
  7. Implementation: Approved boundary changes typically implemented in Fall of following year (Fall 2026)

Factors Considered in Decision-Making

  • Current and future enrollment projections (through 2029)
  • School programs and their impacts (e.g., Dual Language Immersion, Advanced Learner Centers)
  • Facility Condition Index (FCI) - building age and maintenance needs
  • Contiguous boundaries - keeping neighborhoods together

Critical Flaws & Inconsistencies

🚨 Major Data Inconsistency: In-Boundary vs Total Enrollment

The most significant flaw in this study is the disconnect between in-boundary population data and actual total enrollment.

School 2021 In-Boundary Pop 2024 In-Boundary Pop Change 2024-25 Total Enrollment Discrepancy
Morningside 342 309 -10% 561 +82% more than boundary pop
Penn 426 344 -19% 606 +76% more than boundary pop
Eastwood 193 173 -10% 267 +54% more than boundary pop
Oakwood 493 400 -19% 533 +33% more than boundary pop

What This Means: Many schools are heavily reliant on out-of-boundary students (open enrollment, program draws like Morningside's ALC). The study focuses on "resident student forecasts" but actual enrollment is driven by choice-based attendance. Closing a school based solely on declining in-boundary population ignores the reality that these schools are successfully attracting students from outside their boundaries.

⚠️ Facility Condition Index (FCI) - Mixed Picture

Understanding the FCI data for Area 5 schools:

Schools Being Closed:

  • Eastwood: FCI = 4 (Poor/older condition - CLOSING)
  • Oakridge: FCI = 5 (Poor/older condition - Receiving Eastwood students)

Schools Staying Open with Better Condition:

  • Oakwood: FCI = 60 (Best condition in Area 5)
  • Penn: FCI = 58 (Excellent condition)
  • Crestview: FCI = 39 (Good condition)
  • Cottonwood: FCI = 37 (Good condition - declining enrollment)

What This Means:

  • FCI Scale: Higher = Better. FCI 58-60 = excellent/well-maintained. FCI 4-5 = poor/aging.
  • From a facilities perspective, closing lower-FCI schools (Eastwood, Oakridge) makes sense
  • However, this should be balanced against enrollment patterns and community needs

Note: The facilities condition data suggests reasonable building-focused decision-making, though the overall recommendation still has other significant flaws (in-boundary vs. total enrollment mismatch).

⚠️ Data Source Inconsistency

  • Multiple Vendors: Using Davis Demographics (2016-2019), then switching to MGT (2021-2029) creates potential methodological inconsistencies
  • 2020/21 Data Gap: Complete absence of in-boundary population data for 2020/21 makes it difficult to assess COVID-19 impact vs. long-term trends
  • Baseline Uncertainty: Different forecasting methodologies may use different assumptions about birth rates, migration, housing development

⚠️ Capacity Definition Ambiguity

The study uses three different capacity measures but provides only broad ranges:

  • Architectural Capacity: Classrooms × 27.25 (but how many classrooms?)
  • Average Capacity: "Typical number based on programs, labs, preschools, relos, etc." (subjective and varies)
  • Emergency Capacity: Maximum number a building "could accommodate" (undefined)

Problem: Capacity ranges span 100+ students (e.g., "500-550"), making utilization calculations imprecise.

⚠️ Missing Analysis: Economic & Demographic Drivers

The study acknowledges factors influencing enrollment decline but doesn't deeply analyze them:

  • Birth Rate Decline: Mentioned but not quantified for Area 5 specifically
  • Housing Affordability: Area 5 is in the highest-income east side neighborhoods. Have housing prices priced out young families?
  • Charter/Private School Competition: Stated as a factor but no data on how many Area 5 residents attend charters/private schools
  • Open Enrollment Patterns: No analysis of why students choose certain schools

⚠️ Forecast Accuracy Unknown

The study relies heavily on MGT's 2025-2029 forecasts, but:

  • No information provided on MGT's past forecast accuracy for Granite District
  • No sensitivity analysis (what if forecasts are off by ±10%?)
  • COVID-19 disrupted enrollment patterns—are post-2021 trends stabilized or still volatile?

Financial & Resource Considerations

Consolidation Economics

The study argues that consolidation creates "economy of scale" benefits:

10 Schools (Current)

3,980 students ÷ 10 = 398 avg

Many schools under 400 = split-grade classes, limited program funding

8 Schools (PAC Recommendation)

3,980 students ÷ 8 = 498 avg

Most schools reach 500+ = 3 teachers/grade, better resource allocation

Per-Pupil Funding Impact

Smaller Schools = Less Funding for Extras

Elementary schools receive per-pupil funding from:

  • Trust Lands: State-allocated funds based on enrollment
  • TSSA (Teacher & Student Success Act): Per-pupil allocation

A school with 250 students receives half the funding of a school with 500 students, but most staffing costs are fixed (principal, counselor, librarian, specialist teachers, etc.).

Fiscal Comparison to Other Districts

Lesson from Salt Lake City School District (2023)

The presentation cites a 2023 legislative audit criticizing Salt Lake City SD for NOT closing underutilized schools sooner. Key findings:

  • SLC enrollment dropped 17% over a decade, but district rebuilt 3 underutilized schools instead of closing them
  • Cost taxpayers millions in inefficient spending
  • Running underutilized schools cost an extra $3.5 million annually

Granite's Position: The district is attempting to be proactive rather than reactive, addressing declining enrollment before it becomes a fiscal crisis.

Assessing the PAC Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Close Eastwood, Merge with Oakridge

Supporting Evidence Contradicting Evidence
  • Eastwood has lowest in-boundary population (173 current, 131 by 2029)
  • Oakridge has second-lowest (230 current, 194 by 2029)
  • Both schools combined = ~408 students (2029 forecast) = adequate size
  • Contiguous boundaries make merge logical
  • Eastwood has poor building condition (FCI=4 - older/aging infrastructure)
  • From a facilities perspective, closing a lower-FCI school makes some sense
  • HOWEVER: Eastwood total enrollment is 267 (54% higher than boundary pop of 173) - suggests strong program drawing students from across the district
  • The discrepancy between boundary (173) and total (267) enrollment is unexplained - are those 94 out-of-boundary families likely to transfer to Oakridge?

Verdict: This recommendation has mixed merit. Considerations:

  • Facilities perspective: Eastwood has lower FCI (4), so from a building-condition standpoint, closing it is reasonable
  • Enrollment perspective: Eastwood's total enrollment (267) is 54% higher than boundary population (173), suggesting strong programmatic appeal that draws students district-wide
  • Key question: Will those 94 out-of-boundary students follow the merge to Oakridge, or will they leave the district?
  • Alternative: Consider closing Cottonwood instead - it has both the steepest enrollment decline (-42%) AND lower FCI (37)

The recommendation makes sense from a facilities standpoint but needs deeper analysis of the enrollment dynamics.

Recommendation 2: Convert Morningside to French DLI Standalone

Supporting Evidence Contradicting Evidence
  • Morningside boundary enrollment declining (309 current, 253 by 2029 = -18%)
  • French DLI programs need critical mass of students to be viable
  • District wants to maintain two ALC sites on east side (Woodstock + Morningside)
  • Boundary students would move to Rosecrest + Driggs (both have capacity)
  • Morningside total enrollment is 561—one of the highest in Area 5—because of ALC program draw
  • Current boundary residents would lose neighborhood school option
  • Rosecrest and Driggs receive displaced boundary students, but those schools' forecasts don't account for this influx
  • No analysis of how many current Morningside students are in ALC vs. general education vs. French DLI

Verdict: This is the most problematic recommendation. Morningside is NOT underenrolled—it has 561 students. The "problem" is only apparent when looking solely at in-boundary resident data (309 students).

Alternative: Keep Morningside as a dual-track school (neighborhood + ALC + DLI). The building has capacity (~600-650), and total enrollment (561) suggests the community wants the school open.

Alternative Scenarios Not Considered

Scenario 1: Close Cottonwood Instead of Eastwood

Rationale:

  • Cottonwood has the steepest enrollment decline: -42% by 2029 (worst in Area 5)
  • Cottonwood also has lower FCI (37) similar to Eastwood (4)
  • Cottonwood boundary could merge with Oakwood (contiguous, Oakwood has larger capacity and higher FCI=60)
  • This consolidates to a school with better building condition (Oakwood FCI=60)

Challenges:

  • Oakwood is on opposite side of I-215—transportation barriers
  • Cottonwood serves Cottonwood Heights community—politically sensitive
  • Eastwood's out-of-boundary draw (94 students) suggests programmatic strength that might be worth preserving

Scenario 2: Close Oakridge AND Eastwood, Create One Consolidated School

Rationale:

  • Oakridge + Eastwood combined in-boundary pop = ~404 by 2029
  • If total enrollment follows similar out-of-boundary patterns, could be 500-550 students
  • Use Oakridge building (larger capacity ~550-600, FCI=5)
  • Both Oakridge (FCI=5) and Eastwood (FCI=4) have similar lower FCI ratings

Challenges:

  • Closing two schools instead of one is politically harder
  • Larger disruption to two communities
  • Loses potential out-of-boundary enrollment from both schools

Scenario 3: Keep All Schools Open, Accept Lower Per-School Enrollment

Rationale:

  • Community preference for neighborhood schools (as evidenced by feedback that led to Morningside ALC reversal)
  • Area 5 is relatively affluent—PTAs/foundations could supplement funding gaps
  • Keeps buildings in inventory for potential future enrollment growth

Challenges:

  • Continues inefficient use of district resources (as highlighted in SLC School District audit)
  • Split-grade classrooms persist at smaller schools
  • Per-pupil funding disparities—smaller schools have fewer resources for extras
  • Doesn't solve long-term fiscal sustainability if enrollment continues to decline

Critical Unanswered Questions

🔍 Questions the Study Should Address:

  1. Why is there such a large gap between in-boundary population and total enrollment at several schools?
    • Morningside: 309 boundary, 561 total (+82%)
    • Penn: 344 boundary, 606 total (+76%)
    • Eastwood: 173 boundary, 267 total (+54%)
  2. Where are Area 5 boundary residents sending their children if not to boundary schools?
    • Charter schools? Private schools? Open enrollment to other GSD schools? Homeschooling?
  3. What is MGT's historical forecast accuracy for Granite School District?
    • What is the margin of error on 2025-2029 projections?
    • What happens if forecasts are off by 10-15%?
  4. What are the actual per-school operating cost differences?
    • How much does Granite save annually by closing one elementary school?
    • What are the one-time costs of closure?
  5. What is the district's long-term facilities plan?
    • Several schools have poor building condition (FCI 37-60). When do these need major renovations?

Critical Weaknesses

  • 🚨 IN-BOUNDARY VS. TOTAL ENROLLMENT DISCREPANCY: The study's entire foundation rests on declining resident student forecasts, but many schools have significantly higher total enrollment due to choice-based attendance. Morningside (309 boundary vs 561 total), Penn (344 vs 606), Eastwood (173 vs 267) - this 50-80% discrepancy is never adequately explained or accounted for in closure decisions.

Recommendations for Improving the Study

  1. Reconcile In-Boundary vs. Total Enrollment Discrepancies
    • Provide detailed breakdown of where students come from
    • Analyze historical trends: Are out-of-boundary percentages increasing or stable?
    • Survey out-of-boundary families: Why do they choose these schools?
  2. Conduct Forecast Accuracy Analysis
    • Compare MGT's previous forecasts to actual enrollment
    • Provide margin of error and sensitivity analysis
  3. Perform Comprehensive Cost-Benefit Analysis
    • Quantify annual savings from closing one elementary school
    • Include renovation costs for aging buildings
    • Calculate one-time closure costs
  4. Analyze Out-of-Boundary Enrollment Dynamics
    • Survey out-of-boundary families at Eastwood, Penn, and Morningside: Why do they choose these schools over their boundary schools?
    • Predict retention: Will Eastwood's 94 out-of-boundary students transfer to Oakridge or leave the district?
    • Consider whether strong programmatic draws (that create out-of-boundary enrollment) should be preserved rather than consolidated
  5. Clarify Capacity Definitions
    • Provide specific capacity numbers (not ranges)
    • Show current utilization rates using consistent capacity definition

Conclusion

The Granite School District Area 5 Population Analysis Study represents a highly transparent, well-structured process with significant methodological flaws in its core assumptions. The district deserves credit for setting a new standard in public education transparency, but the fundamental analysis has critical gaps.

The district is correct that:

  • District-wide enrollment has declined significantly (-19% since 2010)
  • Projections suggest continued decline through 2029
  • Underutilized schools create fiscal inefficiencies and limit resources for students
  • Proactive planning is better than reactive crisis management

However, the study's critical flaw is its reliance on in-boundary resident forecasts while ignoring the reality of choice-based enrollment. Schools like Morningside (561 students vs. 309 boundary residents) and Eastwood (267 vs. 173) are NOT underenrolled in practice—they're successfully attracting students from beyond their boundaries.

What This Study Does Exceptionally Well:

  • Transparency: 11+ video recordings, individual school data PDFs, AI-summarized feedback—this is model transparency for school districts nationwide
  • Community Engagement: 40-member community subcommittee, multiple feedback mechanisms, extended 12-month timeline
  • Responsiveness: The September 19 ALC reversal proves the district listens when community voices are strong and organized
  • Process Integrity: Follows board policy, includes 19+ departments, considers holistic factors beyond just numbers

What This Study Still Needs to Address:

  • 🚨 MOST CRITICAL: Reconcile in-boundary forecasts with actual total enrollment patterns - the 50-80% discrepancy at several schools undermines closure projections
  • Analyze out-of-boundary enrollment dynamics: Why do families choose schools outside their boundaries? Will they follow consolidations or leave the district?
  • Provide specific cost-benefit analysis with dollar amounts
  • Clarify Morningside status and enrollment breakdowns (ALC vs DLI vs general education)
  • Analyze where boundary residents are actually going (charters, private schools, other GSD schools)

For Community Members:

Final Recommendation: The Board of Education should request additional analysis before finalizing closure decisions in December 2025. The critical missing piece is understanding out-of-boundary enrollment dynamics. The Board should request:

  1. Out-of-boundary enrollment analysis: Survey families choosing schools outside their boundaries - Why do they make these choices? Will they follow consolidations or leave the district?
  2. Enrollment retention projections: For each proposed consolidation, what percentage of current students are expected to stay vs. leave?
  3. Morningside breakdown: How many students are in ALC vs. DLI vs. general education? What happens to each group under the conversion plan?
  4. Cost-benefit analysis with dollar amounts: Annual savings from closures, one-time costs, and comparison to status quo operating costs
